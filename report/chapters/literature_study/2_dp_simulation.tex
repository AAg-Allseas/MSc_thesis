\chapter{DP Simulation}
\label{chapter:DP_simulation}
A dynamic positioning system is a system on board a ship that controls the ship's actuators such that the position of
the ship is kept constant, thus counteracting environmental forces \parencite{duDynamicPositioningShips2018}. This
allows ships to perform operations such as pipe-laying or construction in places where the water depth would otherwise be
restricting. A DP vessel can also reposition much faster than a ship operating on anchors
\parencite{aiModelingSimulationDeepwater2018}.

This chapter will describe the main components of a DP system and outline the mathematical properties of the system.

\section{DP System}
This section will describe the main components that make up a DP system. A global overview will be given, and each
individual component will be further explained.

Most dynamic positioning (DP) systems share a common architecture consisting of four main components: the reference
system, observer, controller, and actuators \parencite{witOptimalThrustAllocation2009}. \Cref{fig:dp_overview}
illustrates these components and their interactions. The overall workflow is as follows: sensors measure the vessel’s
position and velocity; this data is processed by a state estimator to filter out noise and high-frequency motions.
The estimated state is then compared to a desired setpoint to compute the position error. Based on this error, the
controller determines the required force, which is passed to a thrust allocation algorithm that translates it into
actuator commands. Finally, the actuators generate thrust forces that act on the vessel alongside external environmental
forces \parencite{cadetIntroductionKalmanFilter2003}.

\subsection{State estimator}
A ship is subject to a wide range of exciting frequencies. In general, these can be split into high and low frequency
excitations. A DP system aims to compensate just the low frequency motions. The response time of a ships actuators is
generally such that high frequency motions cannot be compensated for. Additionally, filtering saves the actuators from
excessive wear and tear \parencite{serrarisTimeDomainAnalysis2009}. Furthermore, the
position and velocity must be measured with sensors; the measurements are subject to noise. To solve both of these issues, a Kalman filter is generally used. The Kalman filter uses an internal model of the dynamics to predict the state, using
measurements to correct the estimate. As the dynamics of the ship are non-linear, extended Kalman filters are often
used. These linearise the equations of motion around the current estimated state at each timestep. 

The Kalman filter also estimates the so-called “DP current” \parencite{cadetIntroductionKalmanFilter2003}, a virtual
current force which represents the combined effect of all unknown forces acting on the vessel. This state behaves
similarly to the integral term in a Proportional-Integral-Derivative (PID) controller, as it compensates for
steady-state errors by
accounting for persistent disturbances.

\subsection{Controller and thrust allocation}
The controller takes the state estimate and converts it into a desired thruster force. The simplest form of this
controller is a PID controller, though the integral term is often neglected; this function is instead handled by the
state estimator. Other controller architectures also exist.

Once the desired thrust is known, it is passed to a thrust allocation algorithm. Typically, ships have more than one
actuator per degree of freedom, making them over-actuated. This means there are an infinite number of ways to configure
the actuators. To address this issue, an optimisation algorithm is used that aims to match the desired thrust through
actuator settings such as motor RPM and azimuth angle. The objective is to do this with as little power consumption
as possible, while respecting constraints such as forbidden zones, actuator ramp, thrust, and directional rate limitations, and total power consumption per switchboard \parencite{overengDynamicPositioningUsing2021}



\subsection{Actuators}
The actuators are generally defined as any active device producing a force that can be used for station keeping. These
include azimuth thrusters, fixed propellers, rudders, and tunnel thrusters. The actuators are limited by physical
constraints such as delayed responses, ramp-up periods and the rate of directional change. The maximum thrust available is also a
significant limitation. A thruster operating at 100\% capacity is referred to as being saturated. These constraints are
the one of the main reasons time-domain simulations are needed; otherwise, the ship would be able to maintain position almost perfectly
\parencite{borhaugExperimentalValidationDynamic2012}. Thrusters are generally controlled by their revolutions per
minute (RPM), as this is directly measurable. The mapping from RPM to thrust is non-linear and very dependent on the
relative water velocity over the propeller.

Actuators can be subject to numerous disturbances. The main effects that influence the force generated are
thruster-hull interactions, thruster-thruster interactions, and thruster-current interactions
\parencite{serrarisTimeDomainAnalysis2009}. These are difficult to accurately include as these are very complex
phenomena.

\subsection{Vessel Model}
A general manoeuvring model for ships is given by \textcite{fossenHandbookMarineCraft2014}. This model is valid for low-speed manoeuvring. This model generally neglects fluid memory using a zero frequency assumption for the damping and
added mass matrices. An alternative approach uses power-averaged matrices based on the exciting wave spectrum.

Ships on DP are generally viewed in either a body-fixed reference frame or a north-east-down (NED) world-fixed frame.
\Cref{fig:dp_reference_frames} shows the relation between the two. Converting between the two is done with the following formula:

\begin{equation}
    \dot{\eta} = J(\eta)\nu
\end{equation}


Whereby \(\eta\) is the NED position, \(\nu\) is the body-fixed velocity, and \(J(\eta)\) is a rotation matrix mapping the
body-fixed velocity into NED velocity. In the body-fixed rotating reference frame, the equations of motion become the following:

\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:general_eq_motion}
    (M_{RB} + M_A) \dot{\nu} + (C_{RB}(\nu) + C_A(\nu))\nu + (D + D_n(\nu))\nu + G\eta = \tau
\end{equation}

In this formulation, \(M_{RB}\) is the rigid body mass and \(M_A\) is the hydrodynamic added mass. \(D\) is the linear
damping matrix and \(D_n(\nu)\) is the nonlinear damping. The fact that the body-fixed reference frame can be rotating
introduces a Coriolis-centripetal force. This consists of two parts: that caused by rigid body mass \(C_{RB}(\nu)\)
and from hydrodynamic added mass \(C_A(\nu)\). \(\tau\) consists of all the external forces acting on the ship. This is
generally viewed as a linear superposition of environmental forces, actuator forces, and other external forces such as
moorings.

\Cref{eq:general_eq_motion} has several nonlinearities. In the hydrodynamics, the quadratic damping is proportional to
the velocity squared. The thruster forcing is also nonlinear due to the thruster dynamics and Kalman filtering.
Furthermore, the system is causal, dynamic, and time-invariant. 

\section{Environmental Forces}
A significant portion of the external loading comprises environmental forces. In most cases where DP is considered, current is
considered constant, as the timescale at which it changes is significantly longer than that of the system
\parencite{RecommendedPracticeEnvironmental2019}. It can in most cases also be considered irrotational
\parencite{fossenManouveringModels2014}. Both wind and waves are modelled as random processes characterised by energy
density spectra. This results in stochastic loading with known statistical properties.

A stochastic process is a process that, when indexed at time \(t\), does not have a single deterministic trajectory but
rather a set of possible trajectories \parencite{sullivanIntroductionUncertaintyQuantification2015}. The stochastic
nature of the environmental forces results in the DP system also being a stochastic process \parencite{bruvollMultiAgentSystem2013}.
