A dynamic positioning system is a system on board a ship that controls the ship's actuators such that the position of
the ship is kept constant, thus counteracting environmental forces \parencite{duDynamicPositioningShips2018}. This
allows ships to perform operations such as pipe-laying or construction in places where the waterdepth would otherwise be
restricting. A DP vessel can also reposition much faster than a ship operating on anchors
\parencite{aiModelingSimulationDeepwater2018}.

This chapter will describe the main components of a DP system and outline the mathematical properties of the system.

\section{DP System}
This section will describe the main components that make up a DP system. A global overview will be given and each
individual section will be futher explained.

Most dynamic positioning (DP) systems share a common architecture consisting of four main components: the reference
system, observer, controller, and thrusters \parencite{witOptimalThrustAllocation2009}. \Cref{fig:dp_overview}
illustrates these components and their interactions. The overall workflow is as follows: sensors measure the vessel’s
position and velocity, and this data is processed by a state estimator to filter out noise and high-frequency motions.
The estimated state is then compared to a desired setpoint to compute the position error. Based on this error, the
controller determines the required force, which is passed to a thrust allocation algorithm that translates it into
actuator commands. Finally, the actuators generate thrust forces that act on the vessel alongside external environmental
forces \parencite{cadetIntroductionKalmanFilter2003}.

\subsection{State estimator}
A ship is subject to a wide range of exicting frequencies. In general, these can be split into high and low frequency
excitations. A DP system aims to compensate just the low frequency motions, as due to the response time of thrusters the
DP system cannot compensate the high frequency motions \parencite{serrarisTimeDomainAnalysis2009}. Furthermore, the
position and velocity must be measured with sensors; the measurements are subject to noise. To solve both these issues a
Kalman is generally used. The Kalman filter uses an internal model of the dynamics to predict the state, using
measurements to correct the estimate. As the dynamics of the ship are non-linear, extended kalman filters are often
used. These linearise the equations of motion around the current estimated state at each timestep. 

The Kalman filter also estimates the so-called “DP current” \parencite{cadetIntroductionKalmanFilter2003}, which represents the combined effect of all unknown forces acting on the vessel. This state behaves similarly to the integral term in a Proportional-Integral-Derivative (PID) controller, as it compensates for steady-state errors by accounting for persistent disturbances.

\subsection{Controller and thrust allocation}
The controller takes the state estimate and converts this into a desired thruster force. The simplest form of this
controller is a PID controller, though the integral term is often neglected; this function is taken up by the state
estimator. 

Once the desired thrust is known, this is passed to a thrust allocation algorithm. Typically ships have more than one
actuator per degree of freedom, making them over-actuated. This means there are an infinite number of ways to configure
the actuators. To solve this issue an optimisation algorithm is used that aims to match the desired thrust through
actuator settings such as motor RPM and azimuthing angle. The objective is to do this with as little power consumption
as possible, while respecting constraints such as forbidden zones, actuator rate limitations and total power consumption per switchboard. \parencite{overengDynamicPositioningUsing2021}

Actuators can be subject to a large number of disturbances. The main effects that effect the thrust generated are
thruster-hull interactions, thruster-thruster interactions and thruster-current interactions
\parencite{serrarisTimeDomainAnalysis2009}. These are difficult to accurately include, as these are very complex phenomena.

\subsection{Actuators}
The actuators are generally defined as any active device producing a force that can be used for station keeping. These
include azimuthing thrusters, fixed propellers, rudders and tunnel thrusters. The actuators are limited by physical
constraints such as ramp up periods and direction rate limitaions. These constraints are the reason time-domain
simulations are needed; else the ship would be able to maintain almost position perfectly \parencite{borhaugExperimentalValidationDynamic2012}. 

\subsection{Vessel model}
The general non-linear equation of motion for a floating structure can be formulated as follows
\parencite{RecommendedPracticeGlobal2019}:

\[
\label{eq:general_eq_motion}
(M + A(\omega))\ddot{{\mathbf{x}}} + B(\omega)\dot{\mathbf{x}} + D_1\dot{\mathbf{x}} + D_2 \dot{\mathbf{x}} \odot |\mathbf{x}|+ K\mathbf{x} = F^{(1)}_{wave} + F^{(2)}_{wave} + F_{curr} + F_{wind} + F_{ext} + F_{thr}
\]


\[
\begin{split}
    \text{Where:}   \quad & \\
    x               \quad &\text{State of the system}\\
    M               \quad &\text{Structural mass matrix}\\
    A(\omega)       \quad &\text{Frequency dependent added mass matrix }\\
    B(\omega)       \quad &\text{Frequency depended damping matrix}\\
    D_1             \quad &\text{Linear damping matrix}\\
    D_2             \quad &\text{Quadratic damping matrix}\\
    K               \quad &\text{Hydrostatic stiffness matrix}\\
    F^{(1)}_{wave}  \quad &\text{First order wave excitation force}\\
    F^{(2)}_{wave}  \quad &\text{Second order wave excitation force}\\
    F_{curr}        \quad &\text{Current drag force}\\
    F_{wind}        \quad &\text{Wind drag force}\\
    F_{ext}         \quad &\text{External forces}\\
    F_{thr}         \quad &\text{Actuator forces}\\
\end{split}
\]

\Cref{eq:general_eq_motion} has several non-linearities. In the hydrodynamics, the quadratic damping is proportional to
the velocity squared. The thruster forcing is also non-linear due to the thruster dynamics and Kalman filtering.
Furthermore, the system is causal, dynamic and time-invarient. 

\section{Environmental forces}
A significant portion of the external loading are environmental forces. In most cases where DP is considered, current is
considerd constant as the timescale at which it changes is significantly longer than that of the system
\parencite{RecommendedPracticeEnvironmental2019}. Both wind and waves are modelled using energy density spectra. This
results in stochastic loading with known statistical properties. 

A stochastic process is a process that when indexed at time \(t\) does not have a single deterministic trajectory but
rather a set of possible trajectories \parencite{sullivanIntroductionUncertaintyQuantification2015}. The stochastic
nature of the environmental forces result in the DP system also being a stochastic process \parencite{bruvollMultiAgentSystem2013}.
