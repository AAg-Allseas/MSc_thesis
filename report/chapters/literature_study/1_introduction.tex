\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}
Since the introduction of the first ship equipped with dynamic positioning in the 1960s, the use of dynamic positioning
(DP) has become much more widespread in the offshore industry \parencite{HistoryDP2017}. Current industry practice for
making operational decisions is to use quasi-static force balances \parencites
{RecommendedPracticeSpecification2025}{AssessmentStationKeeping2021}. In these calculations, a force balance is found
between environmental loads and thruster forces, neglecting any dynamics of the thrusters. 

When making decisions on board, dynamic positioning operators (DPOs) make use of activity specific operating guidelines
(ASOGs) \parencites{ASOGDecisionSupport2022}{baillyguimaeraesCAMOASOGNaval2023}. These ASOGs contain requirements for
when to cease operations, based on limits such as power or thrust consumption. Component failures are also included in
the ASOGs. These limits are calculated using a quasi-static approach
and do not consider behaviour such as transient excursions in the case of a thruster failing.

Recently, more attention has started going to the use of time-domain simulations to predict dynamic positioning
behaviour \parencites{RecommendedPracticeSpecification2025}{AssessmentStationKeeping2021}.

\subsection{Time-domain DP simulations}
\label{subsec:time_domain_simulations}
When performing time-domain DP simulations, several additional factors affecting the DP behaviour of the ship are
considered. The environmental loads are modelled as time-varying forces, and the thruster dynamics are included. This
allows for a more realistic representation of the DP system, as the thrusters cannot instantaneously provide the
required forces to counteract environmental loads \parencite{armaogluAdvantagesUsingTimeDomain2014}. This allows
time-domain simulations to capture power peaks, the footprint of the ship and transient effects such as thruster failures.

Several studies have been done on the difference between quasi-static calculations and time-domain simulations,
specifically on the difference in predicted operational limits. \Cref{subfig:comparison experiment} shows a
comparison of a dynamic calculation with a static calculation, with experimental data as further context.
\Cref{subfig:comparison failure} shows a comparison of intact and worst-case single failure limits. As \cref{fig:comparison static dynamic dp capability} shows, the quasi-static approach
generally overestimates the limits compared to predictions based on the ships motions. The factor by which is not constant however and ship dependent. As such
dynamic DP simulations are necessary if improved accuracy is desired \parencite{borhaugExperimentalValidationDynamic2012}.

In addition to finding a significant difference between the quasi-static and dynamic capability,
\textcite{borhaugExperimentalValidationDynamic2012} compared their results to experimental data. They found an average
difference of \qty{10}{\%} between the experimental data and their simulations. While this is useful in confirming the
increased accuracy of the time domain approach as opposed to the quasi-static approach, \qty{10}{\%} is still a large
margin for operational decision-making.

Researchers from the Maritime Research Institute Netherlands (MARIN) also developed a time domain DP simulator
\parencite{serrarisDPStationkeepingAccuracy2017}. Their paper highlights that while static DP solvers are useful
when estimating the ultimate capability of a ship, they are unable to say anything about the accuracy of the DP
system. In many operations, such as crane operations, pipelay, drilling or crew transfers
\parencites{tannuriUtilizationNumericalSimulation2011}{serrarisDPStationkeepingAccuracy2017}{theilenNumericalSimulationShip2024},
the maximum excursion can be a limiting factor much before the total power consumption or maximum thrust. The International
Marine Contractors Association (IMCA) updated their recommended practices for evaluating DP behaviour
\parencite{RecommendedPracticeSpecification2025} in 2025. In the revised version, dynamic simulations are recommended for cases
where the footprint is critical, reinforcing the statement from \textcite{serrarisDPStationkeepingAccuracy2017}. 

By using time-domain simulations rather than a quasi-static force balance, more information can be used to determine the
operational limits. These could be defined as the maximum excursion over three hours
\parencite{AssessmentStationKeeping2021} or the probability of exceeding power or thruster limits. The general workflow
for performing time-domain DP simulation is shown in \cref{fig:data_flow_regular}.

    \label{fig:data_flow_regular}
\end{figure}

The starting point is with load characteristics, typically dependent on either a 2D wave spectrum or a 1D spectrum
defined by a significant wave height, \(H_s\), a peak period, \(T_p\), and a wave direction. Using the input
characteristics, a force time series is generated. This force series is passed to a model of the ship together with
model parameters. The model generates a response time series, which is then post-processed to obtain the desired
statistics such as 3-hour maxima for various states. 

\subsection{Probabilistic predictions}
\label{subsec:probabilistic_predictions}
In \textcite{RecommendedPracticeSpecification2025}, the IMCA also warns that time-domain models can contain inaccuracies
due to differences between the model and the actual vessel. Factors influencing the accuracy such as complex interaction
effects, thruster dynamics and controller tuning \parencite{serrarisTimeDomainAnalysis2009} all increase modelling error
and introduce uncertainty into the final predictions. Additionally, environmental loads are subject to uncertainty due
to their stochastic nature \parencite{vanessenRequiredTestDurations2023}, especially when considering more complex wave
spectra such as multi-peaked 2-dimensional spectra \parencite{PioneeringSpiritDP2018}. 

So far, most quantifications of ship DP behaviour use deterministic approaches. These generally use 3-hour simulations 
\parencites{serrarisDPStationkeepingAccuracy2017}{RecommendedPracticeSpecification2025}{AssessmentStationKeeping2021} 
to generate a point forecast with a single set of parameters, which is then post-processed to obtain the desired
statistics, such as 3-hour maxima. In these simulations,
uncertainty in the model or simulation parameters is not considered. Hydrodynamic parameters,
thruster curves, or other such parameters, are rarely known exactly at any given time. Weather forecasts are also
subject to some degree of uncertainty. An example is shown in \cref{fig:hs_measurements}. Here measurements of
significant wave height from two Allseas ships are overlaid on weather forecasts from the UK Met Office. As can be seen,
while the forecasts are quite accurate, there is still some difference between the forecast and the realised significant
wave heights These factors introduce extra uncertainty into the simulations' results. 

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.75\linewidth]{report/images/literature_study/Hs_measurements}
    \caption{Significant wave height measurements from Allseas vs. UK Met Office forecasts}
    \label{fig:hs_measurements}
\end{figure}

Probabilistic forecasting treats uncertain inputs and model parameters as random variables and propagates that
uncertainty through a model to produce a predictive distribution for quantities of interest. Instead of a single point
forecast from one 3-hour run, probabilistic outputs give ranges with associated probabilities, making the risk in
operational decisions explicit \parencite{nowotarskiRecentAdvancesElectricity2018}. 

Quasi-static capability calculation has seen some use of probabilistic forecasting in a study performed by
\textcite{mauroProbabilisticApproachDynamic2022}. A Quasi-Monte Carlo approach was used to investigate the effect of different
significant wave height (\(H_s\)), peak periods (\(T_p\)), and wind speed (\(V_w\)) combinations. Based on the
probability of each combination occurring, a capability plot with confidence interval was generated
(\cref{fig:probabilistic_capability_plot_mauro}). 

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering

        \caption{Probabilistic DP capability plot \parencite{mauroProbabilisticApproachDynamic2022}. Note the capability
        is limited at $\mathcal{X} \in {0, 180}$ as the study considered wind speeds up to a set maximum, whereas the
        reference IMCA considered an unlimited maximum wind speed.}
    \label{fig:probabilistic_capability_plot_mauro}
\end{figure}

This approach required the use of a very large number of samples to converge. \Cref{fig:convergence_history_mauro} shows
the convergence history of their approach in red, with classic Monte-Carlo runs overlaid. The figure shows the number of
Monte-Carlo based samples against an error metric, showing the convergence of the approach.
In both cases, thousands of
runs were needed for the final error value to converge. This method is likely to be prohibitively expensive for use with a
time-domain approach. \textcite{serrarisDPStationkeepingAccuracy2017} mentioned that a single time domain simulation
using the MARIN framework would take approximately 10 minutes; the toy model from \cref{chapter:pilot_tests} took 2.34
minutes per run. To blindly use Monte-Carlo simulations would render the
approach useless for use in offshore decision-making.

Uncertainty modelling and probabilistic forecasting is a well established field
\parencite{gneitingProbabilisticForecasting2014}. It is widely used for applications such as meteorology, economics and
power grids. Particularly power grids are of interest. An example of such an approach applied to a power network is
shown in \cref{fig:eg_prob_forecasting}, where the rotor angle of a generator in a 39-bus system subject to external
stochastic loading is modelled using a surrogate. A power grid is a highly non-linear dynamic system, subject to
external loading. Power distribution is regulated by controllers, aimed at minimising total power consumption
\parencite{sadamotoDynamicModelingStability2019}. Such a system has a lot of parallels to a dynamic positioning system;
a DP system aims to counter external loading while minimising power consumption and setpoint error. Most of these approaches use some form
of surrogate model to more efficiently calculate the dynamic responses of a system, often at the cost of some model
fidelity. 

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.75\linewidth]{report/images/literature_study/yeDeepONetBasedUncertainty2023}
    \caption{Example of probabilistic forecasting in a power network setting \parencite{yeDeepONetBasedUncertainty2023}}
    \label{fig:eg_prob_forecasting}
\end{figure}

In predicting a dynamic system's responses with surrogate models, two main approaches can be considered. The first is to
directly predict output statistics of the system, such as the 3-hour most probable maximum footprint or peak thruster
power, using regression models that map environmental and system parameters to these quantities. The second approach is
to forecast the full time-series, then calculate statistics through post-processing. 

Whilst the direct regression approach is attractive due to its low-dimensional output space and computational efficiency,
experience from nonlinear oscillator and power system surrogate modelling has shown that extreme statistics such as
maxima are highly nonlinear functionals of the time series. Several papers investigated the difference between the two
approaches
\parencites{maiSurrogateModellingStochastic2016}{ebadollahiTimedependentUncertaintyQuantification2025}{yeDeepONetBasedUncertainty2023}.
Directly modelling for maximal responses exhibit large errors, whereas surrogates that reproduce the full time-history, and only
then derive maxima, achieve more accurate predictions of both instantaneous statistics and extremes.

A direct regression on output statistics therefore may compress the complex, non-linear and time-dependent DP dynamics
(\cref{chapter:DP_simulation}) into a single mapping, likely resulting in reduced fidelity, particularly in the
distribution tails relevant for determining operational limits, such as footprint and power margins.

In contrast, a time-series surrogate preserves the dynamic structure of the DP system. This aligns better with current
industrial practice, where dynamic capability and operability assessments are performed in the time domain to capture
low-frequency excursions, transient behaviour after failures, and coupled criteria such as riser tension or dynamic
power load \parencites{smogeliDynCapFullScale}{tannuriUtilizationNumericalSimulation2011}. A key advantage is that the
same surrogate can be reused across operations with different governing limits: for crane operations, acceptance
typically depends on footprint and relative motions, whereas for pipelay or drilling the limiting factors may be
thruster utilisation or generator loading
\parencites{fruzzettiProbabilisticOperabilityGreenhouse2024}{mauroProbabilisticApproachDynamic2022}. Transient events,
such as thruster loss or environmental changes, can also be incorporated directly at the trajectory level, whereas a
statistics-only regression model would require modification or full retraining to reflect such changes.

A final factor influencing the choice between time-series forecasting and direct-regression surrogate models is
explainability \parencite{kamathExplainabilityTimeSeries2021}. When surrogate models directly output aggregated statistics, the reasoning behind the result becomes
harder to trace. In contrast, time-series forecasts provide a full temporal trajectory, making it easier to diagnose
discrepancies and understand model behaviour. This transparency is particularly important in the offshore industry,
where model decisions must be interpretable and auditable for oversight bodies such as class.

Probabilistic forecasting has seen use in predicting dynamic behaviour in electrical grids. Due to the similarities
between the systems,  it is likely to be suitable for predicting DP behaviour. 


\subsection{Uncertainty quantification}
\label{subsec:uncertainty_quantification}
Uncertainty quantification is a field of mathematics that focuses on propagating input uncertainty through a
mathematical system to quantify the uncertainty of the output
\parencite{ebadollahiTimedependentUncertaintyQuantification2025}. Typically, this is done with Monte-Carlo simulations,
but as discussed in \cref{subsec:probabilistic_predictions}, this would be too computationally expensive to blindly use with
dynamic DP analyses. Alternative methods for uncertainty quantification exist. These often consist of some form of
surrogate model to inexpensively model the system's dynamics, which can then be combined with an uncertainty model to
propagate uncertainty from input to output
\parencites{maiSurrogateModellingStochastic2016}{heuvelineHybridGeneralizedPolynomial2014}{nowotarskiRecentAdvancesElectricity2018}. 

Surrogate models can take many forms; for high dimensional non-linear systems machine learning is a promising candidate
\parencite{salinasDeepARProbabilisticForecasting2020}. The use of machine learning models in uncertainty quantification
can lead to significantly faster model evaluations while maintaining decent accuracy
\parencite{willardIntegratingPhysicsBasedModelling2022}.

Uncertainty aware forecasting allows decisions to be made with more information. This makes decisions safer and models
more robust, especially when forecasting out-of-distribution scenarios \parencite{oleksiienkoVariationalNeuralNetworks2023}.

\section{Research question}
\label{sec:research_question}
The marine offshore sector is experiencing a growing interest in the use of time domain simulations to analyse the
dynamic DP behaviour of ships. So far, almost all research done on dynamic positioning has used deterministic
approaches, using realisations of random environmental conditions and accepting modelling errors. Some research has been
done on using probabilistic methods to predict quasi-static DP performance, but these use prohibitively expensive
Monte-Carlo simulations. Probabilistic forecasting using uncertainty quantification has been used in other industries
and shows promise for use with dynamic positioning systems. 

Based on this research gap, the following research question is defined:

\begin{quotation}
    \emph{How can a ship's short-term dynamic positioning behaviour efficiently be forecast while considering environmental and system uncertainty?}
\end{quotation}

To answer this question, a series of research goals will be pursued. The focus of the study will be on generating
uncertainty aware time-series predictions rather than directly obtaining statistics. The method should also be
explainable. In line with this focus, the goals
are as follows: 

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Identify sources and types of uncertainty, and the techniques used to quantify it. 
    \item Identify existing methods for simulating stochastic dynamical systems under uncertainty.
    \item Determine which of these methods are suitable for capturing the stochastic behaviour and model uncertainty.
    \item Implement a selected uncertainty quantification method for short-term time-domain DP forecasting of a vessel, integrating environmental excitation and system dynamics.
    \item Quantitatively compare the proposed probabilistic approach with deterministic and Monte-Carlo-based DP simulations in terms of accuracy, uncertainty representation, and computational efficiency.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Relevance}
\label{sec:relevance}

This section will outline the relevance of this research. It will be split into two categories: academic relevance and
societal relevance. The academic relevance section will outline the contribution to academia and the scientific value of
the method. The societal relevance, on the other hand, is the significance of this research in a broader perspective.

\subsection{Academic}
Existing literature on dynamic positioning prediction focuses on either quasi-static force balances or time domain
simulations. So far, uncertainty and probabilistic forecasting has only been considered in a quasi-static case
\parencite{mauroProbabilisticApproachDynamic2022}. Time-domain simulations remain limited to deterministic realisations
of possible DP behaviour. 

Probabilistic forecasting with uncertainty quantification has been applied in other sectors
such as finance or power networks. Using uncertainty aware forecasting techniques would allow for more robust models as
well as improved interpretability of where errors originate from.

This work will aim to apply probabilistic forecasting methods with uncertainty quantification to DP systems, thereby
filling a gap in existing research. 

\subsection{Societal}
Including uncertainty in time domain DP predictions would provide DP operators significantly more information on which
to base operational decisions. Ensuring these predictions are obtained efficiently would allow on-board forecasting with
actual weather forecasts. This would improve the operability and safety of DP vessels, especially when operating close
to their margins. 

\section{Report Structure}
\label{sec:report_structure}
During the scoping study, the first three goals will be addressed. A literature study will fulfil the first two goals.
This is covered by chapters \ref{chapter:DP_simulation}, \ref{chapter:uncertainty_quantification} and \ref{chapter:dynamic_modelling}.
A pilot test of the methods identified in \cref{chapter:dynamic_modelling} will tackle the third goal in \cref{chapter:pilot_tests}. The last two methods will be the scope of the
following thesis.

First, a general overview of the
dynamic positioning system and existing efforts to simulate it will be given in \cref{chapter:DP_simulation}. This
includes an overview of the system components, mathematical descriptions of the system, and an overview of existing
simulation tools. Next, uncertainty quantification will be explained in \cref{chapter:uncertainty_quantification}. This will start with a
general overview of the concepts behind uncertainty quantification and will then cover commonly used methods. \Cref{chapter:dynamic_modelling} will discuss various methods to handle the simulation of dynamical systems. Two main
concepts will be investigated: latent space models and Neural ODE approaches. Hybrid methods will also be discussed. 

In \cref{chapter:pilot_tests}, a DeepONet and neural SDE (see \cref{chapter:dynamic_modelling}) will be implemented and
compared. Based on these tests a plan is formulated for the subsequent thesis in \cref{chapter:conclusion}.
