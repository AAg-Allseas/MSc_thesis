\chapter{Uncertainty Quantification}
\label{chapter:uncertainty_quantification}



This chapter will investigate the fundamentals of uncertainty quantification. Techniques and methods relevant to the
quantification of uncertainty in DP simulations be identified and documented.

\section{What is Uncertainty Quantification?}
A typical uncertainty quantification problem involves a mathematical model subject to
uncertainty about the correct parameter values \parencite{sullivanIntroductionUncertaintyQuantification2015}.
\textcite{higdonUncertaintyQuantificationError2009} define uncertainty quantification as follows:

\begin{quotation}
    "Uncertainty quantification (UQ) studies all sources of error and uncertainty, including the following: systematic
    and stochastic measurement error; ignorance; limitations of theoretical models; limitations of numerical
    representations of those models; limitations of the accuracy and reliability of computations, approximations, and
    algorithms; and human error. A more precise definition is UQ is the end-to-end study of the reliability of
    scientific inferences"
    \parencite{higdonUncertaintyQuantificationError2009}.
\end{quotation}

In general in the study of UQ, one is interested in the relationship between the input and output. Two general examples
are the forward and the inverse problem \parencite{sullivanIntroductionUncertaintyQuantification2015}. In both examples
we will consider a general model of a physical system:

\begin{equation}
y = f(x)
\end{equation}

In the forward problem, \(f, \, x \) are known. \(y\) can be calculated. However, if \(f\) is an imperfect model or
\(x\) is subject to some uncertainty, then this uncertainty will in some way affect \(y\). This is known as
\textit{forward propagation of uncertainty}.

In the inverse problem, \(y, \, x\) are measured. We are interested in determining \(f\). These measurements are subject to noise, and as \(f\) is a model of a
real system and thus has infinitely many degrees of freedom, the problem is underdetermined. 

For the considered case of a DP system, the forward problem is primarily of relevance, as the dynamics of the system are
known and the effect of uncertainty in the model and inputs is of relevance to the forecasted behavior.

\section{Types of Uncertainty}
In general, sources of uncertainty can be divided into two categories based on their origin. These are aleatoric
uncertainty and epistemic uncertainty \parencite{sullivanIntroductionUncertaintyQuantification2015}. Within any given system, these two forms of uncertainty are not mutually exclusive
\parencite{foxDistinguishingTwoDimensions2011}. 

Aleatoric uncertainty refers to uncertainty inherent in a system. It is irreducible, regardless of the accuracy of the
model or amount of training data. Pure aleatoric uncertainty is understood as the variability observed when repeatedly measuring outcomes from the same process; it reflects the distribution of observed results across all possible outcomes. \parencite{foxDistinguishingTwoDimensions2011}. It is attributed to stochastic behaviour. An example of aleatoric uncertainty could be noise in a measurement sensor. 

Epistemic uncertainty is uncertainty due to a lack of knowledge. Pure epistemic uncertainty can be seen as the
confidence one has in a single observation.
This can be due to uncertainty in parameter values or
in the accuracy of the model. Epistemic uncertainty can be improved by obtaining more data or using a higher fidelity
model. 

When modelling a dynamic positioning system, both forms of uncertainty are present. The system is subject to stochastic
wave and wind loading, as well as noise in sensor measurements. These are sources of aleatoric uncertainty. The fact
that the system is being modelled means epistemic uncertainty is being introduced, as no model will fully capture every
part of the system with complete accuracy. Additional epistemic uncertainty is added in the model parameters, as factors
such as hydrodynamic coefficients are unlikely to be completely accurate at any given time.  

\section{Spectral Methods}
Spectral methods are numerical techniques that represent stochastic processes as series expansions using an orthogonal
basis of functions. They decompose random variables into components, similar to Fourier analysis
\parencite{heuvelineHybridGeneralizedPolynomial2014}. Polynomial Chaos Expansions (PCE) are a common spectral method for
uncertainty quantification.

\subsection{Generalized Polynomial Chaos}
PCE uses polynomials as the orthogonal basis. Originally developed to model Wiener processes using Hermite polynomials, it was extended by
\textcite{xiuWienerAskeyPolynomial2002} to use other polynomials and thus represent other probability distributions. The
generalized polynomial chaos expansion (gPC) works as follows:

Let \(X\) be a random variable in probability space \(\left(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mathbb{P}\right)\), where \(\Omega\) is
the sample space with \(\omega \in \Omega\). \(X\) can then be formulated as follows
\parencite{heuvelineHybridGeneralizedPolynomial2014}:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:gPC_explain_X}
X(\omega) = \sum_{i = 0}^{\infty}c_i\boldsymbol{\Psi}_i(\boldsymbol{\xi})
\end{equation}

\(\boldsymbol{\xi}\) is a random variable subject to some probability distribution. \({\boldsymbol{\Psi}}\) is a set of orthogonal polynomials that describes the
distribution of the random variable. \(c_i\) is a weight given to each term of the polynomial. These are determined by
pointwise sampling at a given point in time. Given a time-dependent process \(X = X(t, \omega)\), \(c_i\) has to be
re-evaluated at each timestep. 

infinite sums are not possible in reality, so the sum is truncated at point \(M\). This gives an approximation of \(X\)
defined as:

\begin{equation}
X^M(t, \omega) \coloneq \sum_{i = 0}^{M}c_i(t)\boldsymbol{\Psi_i}(\boldsymbol{\xi})
\end{equation}
Where M is given by:
\begin{equation}
M = \frac{(P + L)!}{P!L!} - 1
\end{equation}

The truncation order \(M\) is dependent on two discretisation parameters: \(L\) and \(P\). \(P\) is the maximal polynomial order
used, and \(L\) is the dimension of the vector of random variables \(\boldsymbol{\xi} = (\xi_1, \dots, \xi_L)\). The value of M
grows rapidly as the dimensionality of the problem increases. The mean and variance of the result can be determined from
the resulting expansion. 

\begin{align}
    \overbar{X}(t) &= c_0(t) \\
    \mathit{Var} \, \left[ X(t) \right] &= \sum_{i = 1}^{M} c_i(t)^2
\end{align}

Overall, this method works more efficiently than Monte Carlo, as the coefficients for the sum can be estimated using far
fewer model evaluations. Additionally, once the polynomial surrogate model is constructed, it can be evaluated quickly
for any variations of the input values. 

\subsection{Time-Dependent Generalized Polynomial Chaos}
\textcite{gerritsmaTimedependentGeneralizedPolynomial2010} introduced the concept of time-dependent generalized
polynomial chaos (TD-gPC). One of the limitations of gPC is a lack of convergence when the dependence on \(\boldsymbol{\xi}\) is
strongly non-linear. This is especially the case when considering long-term integration
\parencite{heuvelineHybridGeneralizedPolynomial2014}. The difference between this method and the time-independent method
is that, with TD-gPC, the orthogonal polynomials can be adjusted if the distributions of \( \boldsymbol{\xi} \) change.
This increases the accuracy of the PCE over long-term simulations. 

The TD-gPC approach does have some limitations in the application to dynamic systems. For longer simulations, the
accuracy of the method decreases \parencite{gerritsmaTimedependentGeneralizedPolynomial2010}, thus requiring recalculation
and adding extra computing cost. \textcite{ebadollahiTimedependentUncertaintyQuantification2025} also mentions a
deterioration of the performance of TD-gPC for longer simulations. The computational cost rapidly increases as the
required polynomial order increases as time evolves. This makes it inefficient for strongly nonlinear dynamic systems.
The technique does not scale well for higher dimension problems
\parencites{heuvelineHybridGeneralizedPolynomial2014}{maiSurrogateModellingStochastic2016}. 

\subsection{Limitations}

Although spectral methods are powerful for problems with smooth responses and low-dimensional uncertainty, they become
impractical for dynamic positioning systems. DP models involve high-dimensional, nonlinear dynamics and stochastic
environmental inputs, which lead to rapid growth in computational cost and poor convergence for spectral approaches.

Additionally, Polynomial Chaos Expansions (PCE) and other spectral decompositions are typically constructed for a
specific set of uncertain parameters and their probability distributions. For each operating condition and set of
parameters of the DP system, the output distribution can vary significantly. As such, the expansion must be recomputed
or retrained. This means that a tool intended to evaluate behavior under varying conditions would require repeated
recalculation of the PCE; coupled with the curse of dimensionality makes PCE inefficient compared to more flexible approaches such as neural network-based
surrogate models for the desired purpose.

\section{Neural Network Approaches}

This section outlines existing methods used to capture epistemic and aleatoric uncertainty with neural networks. In
general, approaches capture either epistemic or aleatoric uncertainty separately \parencite{galUncertaintyDeepLearning2016}. Epistemic uncertainty is modelled by
placing prior distributions over the weights of the model and capturing how these vary given input data. Aleatoric
uncertainty is quantified by placing a distribution over the output of the model \parencite{kendallWhatUncertaintiesWe2017}

\subsection{Bayesian Neural Networks}
Bayesian neural networks (BNNs) offer a probabilistic extension of standard neural networks, replacing fixed model
parameters with distributions to capture uncertainty in both learning and prediction. This Bayesian treatment provides a
principled framework for understanding model confidence, especially in settings where data are limited, noisy, or shift
over time. Bayes' theorum is the fundamental principle behind BNNs. It is formulated as follows:

\begin{equation}
    P(H \mid D)
    = \frac{P(D \mid H) P(H)}{P(D)}
    = \frac{P(D, H)}{\int_H P(D, H') \, d H'}.
\end{equation}
Here, $H$ denotes a hypothesis (or model), with prior $P(H)$, and $D$ denotes the observed data.
The term
\[
    P(D) = \int_H P(D, H') \, d H'
\]
is called the evidence. The likelihood $P(D \mid H)$ describes how probable the observed data is under the hypothesis, and the posterior $P(H \mid D)$ tells us how likely each hypothesis is after observing the data.

Bayesian neural networks are neural networks trained using this Bayesian approach.
A stochastic neural network is a neural network that uses either stochastic weights or stochastic activation functions.
A BNN is a stochastic neural network trained by Bayesian inference \parencite{mackayPracticalBayesianFramework1992}.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{report/images/literature_study/jospinHandsOnBayesianNeural2022.png}
    \caption{(a) Point-estimate neural network, (b) stochastic neural network with a probability distribution over the activations, and (c) stochastic neural network with a probability distribution over the weights. \parencite{jospinHandsOnBayesianNeural2022}}
\end{figure}

The stochastic model is chosen by the user and consists of a prior distribution over possible model parameterizations and a prior confidence.
In the notation above, these correspond to $H$, with the training data playing the role of $D$.
The choice of prior is an architectural decision similar to the choice of loss function in point-estimate networks.
The goal of training is to obtain the posterior $p(\theta \mid D)$, the probability distribution of the weights $\theta$ given the observed data.

The standard procedure is to use a Gaussian distribution over the weights as the prior
\parencite{jospinHandsOnBayesianNeural2022}, though Gaussian priors are not necessarily better than other choices
\parencite{silvestroPriorChoiceAffects2020}.

When using a BNN for prediction, the marginal predictive distribution $p(y \mid x, D)$ quantifies the uncertainty in the prediction.
In practice, this can be approximated by sampling weight configurations from the posterior $p(\theta \mid D)$ and evaluating the network, yielding a set of predictions that follow $p(y \mid x, D)$.

BNNs quantify epistemic uncertainty through the posterior over weights $p(\theta \mid D)$ and aleatoric uncertainty through the data noise encoded in the likelihood $p(y \mid x, \theta)$ \parencite{jospinHandsOnBayesianNeural2022,kendallWhatUncertaintiesWe2017}.
In practice, the evidence term $P(D)$ is not feasible to calculate exactly, meaning the posterior cannot be obtained in closed form.
To address this, two main families of approximate inference methods are used: Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) and variational inference (VI).

MCMC constructs a Markov chain whose stationary distribution matches the desired posterior distribution.
A common algorithm, the Metropolis-Hastings algorithm, starts from an initial random guess and then repeatedly samples new candidate points around the current state from a proposal distribution.
The candidate is accepted with a probability given by the ratio of the target density at the candidate point and at the current point.
Other algorithms, such as Hamiltonian Monte Carlo, exist to improve the efficiency of this process.
Because successive samples are autocorrelated, the chain must be subsampled, which increases the number of samples required.
MCMC approaches also suffer from a burn-in period, during which the initial samples do not yet follow the target distribution.
Finally, designing efficient proposals becomes increasingly challenging in higher-dimensional parameter spaces.

Variational inference works by introducing a variational distribution $q_\phi(\theta)$ parameterised by $\phi$, and then optimising $\phi$ so that $q_\phi(\theta)$ matches the true posterior $p(\theta \mid D)$ as closely as possible.
The discrepancy between $q_\phi$ and the exact posterior is commonly measured using the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence.
Directly minimising this KL divergence would require access to the exact posterior, which is unavailable.
Instead, one maximises the evidence lower bound (ELBO), which can be written as the log evidence minus the KL divergence; maximising the ELBO is therefore equivalent to minimising the KL divergence
\parencite{kingmaAutoEncodingVariationalBayes2014,blundellWeightUncertaintyNeural2015}.
In practice, this is generally done using stochastic gradient descent, treating the negative ELBO as a loss function.

However, naive stochastic gradients suffer from high variance due to the stochasticity in sampling from $q_\phi(\theta)$.
To address this, methods such as Bayes by Backprop
\parencite{blundellWeightUncertaintyNeural2015} use the reparameterisation trick
\parencite{kingmaAutoEncodingVariationalBayes2014} to express samples from $q_\phi(\theta)$ as deterministic functions of fixed noise variables and the parameters~$\phi$.
This moves the stochasticity outside the main computational graph, allowing unbiased, low-variance gradients of the ELBO to be propagated back through the network.

BNN in practice suffer from high computational costs during training and inference due to the sampling required. This
often makes them unsuitable for use in large, high dimensional systems
\parencite{willardIntegratingPhysicsBasedModelling2022}. Furthermore, variational inference is known to underestimate
uncertainty \parencite{kruegerBayesianHypernetworks2017}.

\subsection{Monte Carlo Dropout}
Dropout is a technique generally used to regularise neural nets to improve their robustness and generalisability. It is
usually used during training and works by randomly enabling or disabling a set number of neurons.
\textcite{galDropoutBayesianApproximation2016} showed that by enabling dropout not just during training allows the network to
approximate a distributional posterior. Resampling the model multiple times with different dropout activations
essentially results in sampling multiple similar models, each with their own parameterisation of the problem. This
results in a distribution of output predictions, thus quantifying the uncertainty in the model.

The theoretical basis is that dropout can be interpreted as variational inference over a BNN where
the variational distribution is a Bernoulli distribution \parencite{galDropoutBayesianApproximation2016}. This allows
the model to give a bayesian average without training a full ensemble. This allows the model to quantify epistemic
uncertainty. An alternative interpretation of this approach is that dropout results in an ensemble of neural networks
with shared parameters \parencite{lakshminarayananSimpleScalablePredictive2017}. 

In practice, the approach is simple to implement as dropout is often already used for training regularisation. The only
change needed to the architecture to enable dropout is adding dropout layers between the hidden layers
\parencite{wabinaNeuralStochasticDifferential2023}. The approach is fast and scales well compared to BNNs, and still
gives usable results. The prediction of the uncertainty is limited however, as the Bernoulli variational distribution is restrictive. This
limits its ability to capture the true posterior distribution of the network. The quality of the uncertainty
quantification is also sensitive to the dropout rate; the rate must therefore be tuned to get the desired behavior.
Finally, by disabling parts of the network, the quality of each individual prediction will be worse. Overall, this
method of uncertainty quantification is the crudest of the neural network approaches discussed in this section.

\subsection{Hypernetworks}

A hypernetwork is a network used to approximate the posterior of the weights of a network of interest. It does this by
sampling random noise and outputting a sample from the approximate posterior of the network in the form of a set of
weights and biases \parencite{kruegerBayesianHypernetworks2017}. The major advantage hypernetworks have over BNNs is
that the posterior they estimated can be arbitrarily complex as opposed to being fit by variational distributions. This
means the uncertainty quantification is much more accurate than other methods discussed, without the cost of MCMC. This
is particularly useful when the system expresses strong multi-modality as this would likely be underestimated by other
approaches.

In practice however hypernetworks tend to be expensive to train and sample as the full set of weights of the model
network have to be generated each iteration. While the accuracy of the uncertainty estimate can theoretically exceed
deep ensembles, real examples have shown they tend to perform similarly for capturing epistemic uncertainty.

\subsection{Deep ensembles}

Deep ensembles are a method to quantify epistemic uncertainty. Proposed in
\citeyear{lakshminarayananSimpleScalablePredictive2017} by
\citeauthor{lakshminarayananSimpleScalablePredictive2017} as a method for uncertainty quantification, they work by training multiple of the same neural network
from different random initial weights and biases. At inference time, each of these networks is sampled. If the networks are in
agreement the epistemic uncertainty is low, wheras if the networks disagree the epistemic uncertainty is high. 

The approach is simple and requires minimal modifications to the neural networks architecture to implement. The quality
of the uncertainty quantification is high and the computation cost at inference time is low, as only a relatively small
number of samples are needed compared to the other approaches discussed.
\textcite{lakshminarayananSimpleScalablePredictive2017} found that random shuffling of the dataset and random
initialisation of the weights was sufficient to obtain good results. This reinforces the observation
\textcite{leeWhyHeadsAre2015} made while investigating how to create a diverse set of ensemble networks.

A major limitation of the deep ensemble approach is that the cost of training is high, as the same model needs to be
trained repeatedly. For an ensemble of five networks, the training cost is fivefold.
